Police Legislation Book

This legislation book contains the core laws and powers officers are expected to know and apply.

Legislation book link: Open the Police Legislation Book

How to Use

  • Use this as your reference for powers and offences when writing up MDT reports.
  • If you’re unsure which power to cite for a search, also review Search Powers.

Key Acts & Sections (Quick Reference)

Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (PACE)

  • Section 1: Stop and search (person/vehicle) with reasonable grounds.
  • Section 17: Entry of a property (without warrant) to arrest or save life/limb/property.
  • Section 18: Search premises after arrest (authorised by on-duty Inspector+).
  • Section 19: Power of seizure (prevent loss/destruction of evidence).
  • Section 24: Powers of arrest.
  • Section 32: Search upon arrest (danger to self/others; includes certain related searches).

Police Reform Act 2002

  • Section 50: Power to acquire name and address (anti-social behaviour).

Public Order Act 1986

  • Section 4: Harassment, alarm and distress (abusive behaviour).
  • Section 5: Harassment, alarm, distress (threatening/abusive behaviour).

Mental Health Act

  • Section 136 — Mental Health Act 1983: If someone appears to be in a mental health crisis in a public place, police can take them to a place of safety for assessment (normally healthcare setting). Sectioned suspects should be in medical custody or a holding cell with nothing to cause harm. Can last up to 72 hours (adjustable by medical/authorisation).

Road Traffic Act 1988

  • Section 4: Under the influence of drink/drugs while in charge of a motor vehicle (arrest power; roadside tests).
  • Section 5: Driving over prescribed alcohol limits (80mg/100ml blood, 35mg/100ml breath, 107mg/100ml urine).
  • Section 163: Stop a moving motor vehicle on public roads.
  • Section 164: Gather driving documents.
  • Section 165: Obtain name and addresses / insurance details.
  • Section 165A: Seize a vehicle if driven illegally.

Misuse of Drugs Act 1971

  • Section 3: Importation/exportation offences.
  • Section 4: Production/supply offences.
  • Section 23: Power to search for controlled substances.
  • Section 24: Powers to arrest for drug offences.

Criminal Damage Act 1971

Criminal damage involves destroying/damaging property belonging to another (or shared), done maliciously with intent or recklessness.

Assault (Criminal Justice Act 1988)

Assault examples include: spitting, slapping, smacking, pushing, or any use of force to cause harm to another individual.

ABH (Actual Bodily Harm): Intentional or reckless assault causing actual bodily harm; the assault must have “occasioned” the harm.

GBH (Grievous Bodily Harm): Really serious injuries (e.g., broken bones, permanent disfigurement). GBH is covered by Sections 18 and 20 of the Offences Against the Person Act 1861.
